CARB J1667 Smoke Testing Explained: California's Heavy-Duty Standard

Published September 10, 2025 · NorCal CARB Mobile

J1667 Smoke Testing Explained: California's Heavy-Duty Standard for older diesel engines.

What Every Heavy-Duty Fleet Owner Needs to Know About J1667 Testing

If you operate heavy-duty diesel vehicles in California, you've likely heard about J1667 smoke testing. But understanding exactly what it entails—and why it's critical for your fleet's compliance—can mean the difference between smooth operations and costly violations.

As California's emissions standards become increasingly stringent, J1667 smoke testing has emerged as the gold standard for heavy-duty vehicle compliance. Here's everything you need to know.

Understanding J1667: The Technical Foundation

J1667 refers to the Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) standard for snap-acceleration smoke testing of heavy-duty diesel vehicles. This isn't just bureaucratic paperwork—it's a precise scientific measurement that determines whether your vehicles meet California's strict opacity standards.

What Makes J1667 Different

Unlike basic visual inspections, J1667 testing uses calibrated opacity meters to measure the exact percentage of light blocked by exhaust smoke during acceleration. California requires that diesel vehicles produce no more than:

  • 20% opacity for most heavy-duty vehicles

  • 15% opacity for vehicles with Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR) over 14,000 lbs manufactured after 1991

  • 40% opacity during the acceleration peak (lasting no more than 3 seconds)

The J1667 Testing Process: What to Expect

Pre-Test Vehicle Preparation

Before testing begins, our certified technicians ensure your vehicle meets specific conditions:

  • Engine at normal operating temperature

  • Vehicle positioned on level ground

  • All emission control systems functioning

  • Engine idle speed verified

The Snap-Acceleration Test

The actual J1667 procedure involves:

  1. Baseline Reading: Recording ambient light conditions

  2. Acceleration Sequence: Rapidly accelerating the engine from idle to governed speed

  3. Opacity Measurement: Recording maximum opacity during the acceleration event

  4. Multiple Runs: Conducting three separate tests for accuracy

  5. Pass/Fail Determination: Comparing results against California standards

Total testing time: Approximately 15-20 minutes per vehicle

Why California Mandates J1667 Testing

Environmental Protection

Heavy-duty diesel vehicles are significant contributors to particulate matter (PM) emissions. The J1667 standard helps ensure that:

  • Diesel particulate filters (DPF) are functioning properly

  • Engine combustion remains efficient

  • Emission control systems haven't been tampered with

Public Health Compliance

California's stringent standards directly impact air quality in major metropolitan areas. J1667 testing helps protect public health by:

  • Reducing respiratory irritants

  • Minimizing particulate pollution

  • Supporting the state's climate goals

Who Needs J1667 Testing?

Mandatory Testing Categories

J1667 smoke testing is required for:

  • Commercial diesel vehicles over 14,000 lbs GVWR

  • Fleet vehicles subject to CARB regulations

  • Buses and motorcoaches

  • Heavy-duty trucks operating in California

Testing Frequency

Most vehicles require testing:

  • Annually for compliance verification

  • Upon registration in California

  • After major engine work or emission system repairs

  • During random CARB inspections

Common J1667 Testing Failures and Solutions

Dirty Air Filters

Problem: Restricted airflow causes incomplete combustion Solution: Replace air filters before testing

Faulty DPF Systems

Problem: Clogged or damaged diesel particulate filters Solution: Professional DPF cleaning or replacement

Engine Timing Issues

Problem: Incorrect injection timing increases opacity Solution: Professional engine diagnostics and adjustment

Poor Fuel Quality

Problem: Low-quality diesel increases particulate emissions Solution: Use premium diesel fuel and additives
